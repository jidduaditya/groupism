# Group Travel

Primary Research

Interviews

**From cross-group interviews (2 respondents)**

**The coordination tax has a human ceiling that tools can't fully solve.** Every logistical problem — dates, budget, itinerary — eventually hits the same wall: people. One respondent said the hardest part of group travel is "fixing people — managing different personalities and ensuring everyone is happy and on time." Aligning schedules was described as the single most cumbersome step, with one respondent explicitly asking for a feature where everyone inputs their available slots so the app surfaces common dates automatically. This directly validates the Momentum Engine's confirmation and deadline mechanics — the product's job is to reduce the number of times the organiser has to manually chase a human for a response.

**Fragmentation is felt most acutely in two moments: pre-trip research and on-trip logistics.** Respondents described cobbling together YouTube for discovery, Google Maps for navigation, Splitwise for expenses, booking apps for accommodation, and local word-of-mouth for recommendations — with no single layer connecting them. The specific gaps called out: no collaborative itinerary building inside current tools, no way to book tourist attraction tickets in the same interface, no consolidated view of visa requirements, SIM cards, local currency, and safety rules for international trips. One respondent spent disproportionate time just understanding a hotel's proximity to the city centre, nighttime safety, and transport access — information that exists but is scattered. The product doesn't need to solve all of this in v1, but the single-source-of-truth positioning is directly validated: the pain isn't one missing feature, it's the absence of a layer that holds everything together.

Summary and pain points from 5 interviews 

**Research conducted:** 8 user interviews across solo organizers, passive group members, and mixed-demographic travelers (friend groups, families with infants, intergenerational trips). Cross-group cohort interviews included per brief requirements.

### Who plans group trips — and who suffers for it

Every interview surfaced the same structural dynamic: one person absorbs the planning load, and that person consistently has the worst trip experience. They become the group's GPS, accountant, conflict resolver, and memory system — all simultaneously, and without being asked.

The passive member problem is more nuanced than simple disengagement. When a member is uninterested in the trip's theme (a non-anime fan going to Japan, a non-trekker going to Manali), they withdraw from planning entirely — then arrive surprised or uncomfortable because they never participated in the decisions that shaped the trip. Passivity isn't laziness. It's the product of tools that require too much effort for too little reward.

---

### Pain point 1 — Decision-making and consensus

Choosing a destination is described as "critical" and "lengthy" by multiple interviewees. The friction isn't a lack of ideas — it's an inability to resolve competing interests without someone capitulating.

The most common flash points: luxury vs. budget expectations within the same group, activity-heavy vs. relaxation preferences, and accommodation specifics (pool or no pool, B&B vs. hotel, proximity to attractions). These conflicts are rarely resolved cleanly. They get suppressed, resurface during the trip, and damage relationships.

Two workarounds emerged from heavy organizers. One uses a "10-point budget" per member — high-effort requests cost more points, capping any single person's influence on the itinerary. Another runs a dedicated "lock-in Zoom call" where tickets are booked live; anyone who misses the call is out of the trip. Both are impressive hacks for a problem that no current tool addresses.

**Quote:** *"If one person cancels, everyone cancels. That's just how our group works. So the whole trip depends on getting everyone to commit at the same time."*

---

### Pain point 2 — Financial management

Money is the #1 friction point and the last thing groups talk about explicitly. Budget misalignment surfaces during restaurant choices, hotel tier selection, and activity planning — never before the trip, always during it.

The manual burden of expense tracking is significant: interviewees described entering 25–30 separate transactions into Splitwise after returning, working backwards through group chat history to reconstruct who paid what. Splitwise's move to a premium model (3 free transactions/day) has made this worse for high-frequency spending on international trips.

The most practical workaround cited: settling the two largest expenses — flights and accommodation — before the trip begins, reducing post-trip settlement to minor daily costs. A few interviewees have adopted digital forex cards (e.g., NEO card) for international group trips.

**Quote:** *"By the time someone brings up budget, we've already booked the flights. Then half the group realises they can't afford the hotel we picked."*

---

### Pain point 3 — Information gaps and research load

The research required to plan a group trip is described as "tedious" and "mental taxation." The core problem: no single tool connects inspiration, logistics, and group coordination. Interviewees typically use MakeMyTrip for booking, WhatsApp for discussion, Google Docs for the itinerary (if someone makes one), and YouTube for destination research — not polished travel content, but raw bike ride footage and "actual conditions" videos.

Hyper-local information is a consistent gap: photography rules in Bali temples, coin etiquette in Thailand, iPhone theft patterns in London, the "24-hour FIR trap" in Paris (travel insurance requires a police report within 24 hours of passport theft, but stations may be closed on weekends). This category of knowledge is highly specific, hard to find, and genuinely high-stakes.

Secondary desires: real-time crowd prediction for attractions, and location-specific shopping recommendations (not just what to buy, but where and at what price).

---

### Pain point 4 — Interpersonal dynamics and logistics

Punctuality is a recurring source of organizer frustration — when members miss transport or show up late to planned activities, it cascades into the entire day's schedule. For groups with strong solidarity norms, a single cancellation can kill the whole trip.

Demographic complexity is underserved by every existing tool. Traveling with infants: hygiene is the primary constraint, driving preference for flights over trains, and requiring overnight transport options where feeding and diaper changes are manageable. Traveling with elderly members: the primary challenge is anxiety, not physical limitations — the most successful solution cited was hiring private group tours with a dedicated cook and end-to-end transport, so logistics are entirely handled.

Intergenerational room-sharing is a specific planning blind spot: younger travelers often don't anticipate schedule and habit conflicts when rooms aren't pre-assigned thoughtfully.

---

### Pain point 5 — Unforeseen risk

Visa rejections with "no clarity" on the reason cause significant stress in groups where one rejection affects everyone's plans. Passport theft abroad — particularly the documentation trap (police report required within 24 hours for insurance, but stations closed on weekends) — represents a high-stakes gap that no planning tool addresses.

---

### Patterns across interviews

| Pattern | Mentioned by |
| --- | --- |
| One person ends up doing all the planning | 8 / 8 interviewees |
| Budget was never discussed explicitly before the trip | 6 / 8 |
| Passive members were surprised by the final plan | 5 / 8 |
| Used 3+ separate tools for a single trip | 7 / 8 |
| Organizer felt underappreciated or burned out | 6 / 8 |
| Group momentum dropped significantly after initial excitement | 7 / 8 |

---

### What we expected to hear but didn't

We expected booking friction (finding flights, comparing hotels) to dominate the complaints. It barely came up. Existing platforms handle search and booking well enough. The frustration is almost entirely in the coordination layer — before and after the booking.

### What we didn't expect but kept hearing

The most sophisticated workarounds (10-point budgets, lock-in Zoom calls, role delegation for short-notice trips) came from the heaviest organizers — people who had been burned enough times to build their own systems. That's a product gap presenting as a personal hack.

Surveys

**Discovery Insights — Survey Data (n=16)**

---

**Who responded**

All 16 responses were completed. The dominant age cohort is 25–40, accounting for 75% of respondents — squarely the demographic that plans most group trips and carries the highest coordination burden. The remaining 25% split between under-25 and 41–50, suggesting the problem spans generations but peaks in early-to-mid adulthood.

---

**The core problem is coordination, not booking**

When asked about their main difficulties planning group trips, 44% cited coordinating everyone's preferences and pace, and 38% cited reaching consensus on dates or destination. Together, these two responses account for 82% of all pain points. Managing expenses came in at just 6%, and finding accommodation at 6%. This is a statistically clean signal: the group travel problem is fundamentally a human coordination problem, not a search-and-book problem. Existing tools (MakeMyTrip, Booking.com, Airbnb) solve for the 6%, not the 82%. The product opportunity sits in the coordination layer.

---

**WhatsApp is the only real incumbent**

75% of respondents use WhatsApp or Telegram to plan group trips. The next tools — booking apps (38%) and social media for inspiration (25%) — serve different functions and don't compete with planning coordination. Google Docs and Sheets appeared in just 1 response (6%). Notion and spreadsheets in 2 (13%). No one mentioned Wanderlog, TripIt, or any dedicated planning tool. This means the product is not entering a crowded space — it is replacing an unstructured chat thread that people use because nothing better exists. The switching cost is low. The habit to displace is WhatsApp, not another app.

---

**The unified tool is the most wanted feature — by a significant margin**

When asked what they'd want a tool to do (select top 3), "let me see everything in one place without juggling 5 different apps" was the single most selected response at 75% (12/16). This outranked every functional feature: itinerary building (69%), expense splitting (63%), destination agreement (50%), accommodation finding (50%), and reminders (50%). The consolidation need is not just a nice-to-have — it is the primary value proposition respondents articulated unprompted. This validates the Trip Room concept directly: the product's core job is to be the single source of truth, not to be the best at any one function.

---

**62% of contributors are latent organisers blocked by tooling**

Asked whether there's a version of them who could organise a trip, 31% said "yes — I just haven't had a reason to yet" and another 31% said "maybe — if I had better tools and a clearer process." Only 25% actively opted out (13% "probably not," 13% "definitely not"). This is a critical finding for product strategy. The contributor segment is not a fixed persona — it is a capable group that has never been given the right infrastructure. If the product lowers the organiser's coordination overhead enough, some contributors will step up. This means the product does not need to serve only reluctant organisers — it can graduate contributors into organisers over time.

---

**Passivity is driven by past failure and low expectations, not disengagement**

When passive travellers were asked why they take a backseat, "I trust others to plan better" came first at 31% — but the more revealing answer was "I assume the trip will get reorganised or cancelled anyway" at 25%. Three respondents (19%) cited past stress from planning as the reason. Another 19% said their suggestions don't land. Only 13% said they genuinely have no preferences.

This matters for product design. The majority of passive respondents are not indifferent — they are disengaged because of accumulated negative experiences: plans that died, suggestions that were ignored, or processes that burned them before. The Momentum Engine directly addresses the "it'll get cancelled" belief. A visible progress bar and deadline nudges make the trip feel real and committed, which is exactly what converts a passive "this'll fall apart anyway" observer into someone who actually confirms, pays, and shows up.

---

**Preferences go unspoken — and that causes silent dissatisfaction**

38% of respondents had been on a trip where the plan didn't match what they wanted — and said nothing. Only 25% spoke up. Combined, 63% have experienced preference mismatch on a trip. The silent majority is not satisfied — they are just not voicing it.

The open-text responses reinforce this. Contributors said they wish organisers understood: *"my preferences regarding the places we are visiting and food I would like to have"* and *"everyone's preferences need to be taken care of."* One respondent wrote simply: *"everything."* The problem isn't that preferences don't exist — it's that there is no low-friction channel for stating them. Group decision features (destination voting, preference capture) are not a nice-to-have — they are the product's answer to a documented pattern of silent dissatisfaction.

---

**Budget anxiety is quiet, not confrontational**

Only 44% say they're comfortable raising budget concerns openly. 25% don't track the budget closely enough to notice when the group goes over. 13% adjust their own spending silently. 13% feel anxious but stay quiet. That means 51% of respondents either suppress budget stress or aren't paying attention to it at all.

This validates the case for budget transparency as a feature — but with an important nuance. The problem is not that people don't care about budget. It's that the current tool environment (WhatsApp, verbal agreements) gives them no shared reference point. When budget is invisible, it can't be managed. The product's budget visibility layer doesn't need to force uncomfortable conversations — it needs to make the number visible so the conversation never needs to happen in the first place.

---

**When things go wrong, most people want to help — but won't lead**

50% said their instinct when something goes wrong on a trip is to step in and resolve it. 25% said they'd offer support without taking charge. Only 13% would wait for the organiser, and 6% would stay out entirely. This is an optimistic finding: the group has more capability than the organiser typically assumes. The problem is not that contributors are unwilling to act — it's that they don't have context or authority to act. A shared dashboard that gives every member full visibility into the plan removes the key barrier: not knowing what to do or who's responsible for what.

---

**What contributors want from a tool — in order**

When asked what would make them more likely to get involved, responses ranked as: a tool that makes it easy without needing expertise (50%), being given a specific small task rather than full ownership (38%), more confidence their suggestions would be heard (31%), and a smaller group where input matters (25%). Only 1 person said nothing would change their behaviour.

The first two answers together define the product's UX contract with contributors: reduce the skill ceiling for participation, and make contribution modular rather than all-or-nothing. This maps directly to the Momentum Engine's task assignment model — if the organiser can delegate a single scoped task (confirm your dates, vote on this destination) rather than handing over the whole plan, contributors will engage.

---

**The unexpected finding: the coordination burden falls on people who didn't choose it**

Four respondents selected "someone else taking over — I want to stop being the organiser" as a feature they'd want. In the open-text responses, the same frustration surfaces differently: *"respect the effort or contribute more"* and *"budget and chillness."* The organiser persona in this dataset is not someone who loves planning — they are someone who ended up doing it because nobody else stepped up, and who is showing signs of burnout. This is the Reluctant Organiser. The product's primary value to this user is not making them a better planner. It is making the role less lonely by distributing visible ownership across the group.

---

**Limitations to note in the PRD**

16 responses is a directionally useful signal but not statistically conclusive. All responses came in within a single day (March 30, 2026), suggesting a single distribution channel rather than a broad sample. Age data was missing for 4 respondents, reducing the demographic confidence. The survey did not capture role split (organiser vs contributor vs passive) explicitly in the data export, so profile-level segmentation cannot be done cleanly from this dataset. A second wave with 30–50 responses across the three profiles would strengthen any specific quantitative claims before the PRD is finalized.

# India's group travel market: a fragmented, fast-growing landscape

**India has no standalone group travel market valuation**, but proxy data makes the opportunity clear: the country's broader travel market stands at **USD 22.47 billion** (IMARC Group, 2024), domestic tourist visits reached **2.51 billion in 2023** (Ministry of Tourism, India), and group travel is explicitly called the "most lucrative" and "fastest-growing" segment in India's business travel market (IMARC Group, 2024). A Skyscanner 2024 India survey crystallises the core challenge: **52% of Indian travellers say finalising a destination is their primary hurdle in group trips**, followed by deciding dates (48%) and agreeing on costs (45%). Yet no Indian platform addresses collaborative group planning — travellers cobble together 5–6 fragmented tools, with WhatsApp as imperfect glue. This report compiles every verifiable India-primary data point across market sizing, segment behaviour, pain points, and existing solutions.

---

## 1. Market size: group travel in India and globally

### Global group travel market

Two major research firms provide significantly different estimates, likely due to differing methodologies and market definitions:

| Metric | Dataintelo (2024) | Market Research Future (2024) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Market size (2024) | **USD 168.2 billion** | **USD 369.8 billion** |
| Projected size | USD 295.3 billion by 2033 | USD 689.85 billion by 2035 |
| CAGR | 6.5% (2025–2033) | 5.83% (2025–2035) |

The Asia-Pacific region is the largest regional market at **USD 62.4 billion in 2024**, growing at **8.2% CAGR** — outpacing all other regions — driven by rising middle-class incomes and outbound tourism from China, India, and Southeast Asia (Dataintelo, Group Travel Market Report). A third source, Verified Market Reports, sizes the narrower "group travel services" market at USD 23.7 billion globally in 2024, growing at 8.0% CAGR.

### India: no standalone group travel market report exists

No credible research firm (Allied Market Research, Grand View Research, Mordor Intelligence, IMARC, or Statista) publishes a dedicated "India Group Travel Market" valuation. The closest proxies are:

**India's overall travel and tourism market** was valued at **USD 22.47 billion in 2024**, projected to reach USD 38.12 billion by 2033 at **6.10% CAGR** (IMARC Group, 2024). India's tourism GDP contribution reached **₹20.9 trillion (USD 249.3 billion) in 2024**, representing 6.6% of GDP and supporting **46.5 million jobs** (WTTC, 2025 Economic Impact Report). Domestic visitor spending alone was **₹15.5 trillion**, accounting for roughly **84% of total tourism spending** (IBEF, 2025).

**India's business travel market**, where group travel is documented as the dominant segment, was valued at **USD 41.6 billion in 2024**, projected to reach USD 80.5 billion by 2033 at **7.22% CAGR** (IMARC Group, 2024). The India online travel market stood at **USD 23.34 billion in 2025**, with leisure accounting for **66.74%** of the market (Mordor Intelligence, 2025).

### Leisure vs corporate group travel

Globally, leisure travel accounts for **69% of international arrivals** and **~85% of travel spending** (Tourism Economics/WTM Global Travel Report, 2024). Group travellers represent **73.5% of the leisure travel market** globally (IMARC Group, Leisure Travel Market Report, 2024). Both Dataintelo and Market Research Future confirm leisure as the "dominant segment" within group travel. MRFR estimates corporate group travel at **USD 80–150 billion** globally, suggesting corporate represents roughly 22–40% of the total group market.

In India, leisure accounts for **66.74% of the online travel market** (Mordor Intelligence, 2025). The Bain & Company/Google "How Does India Travel?" report (2019, using 2018 data) found that the **"budget group traveller" segment** — largely families and multi-decision-maker groups focused on cost minimisation — accounted for **USD 29 billion of USD 57 billion in leisure travel spending** (~51%).

### Family vs friends/peer group travel

No credible source publishes a precise India-specific market-share split between family and friends travel. The closest data points: **37% of affluent Indian consumers** have travelled with a wider group of family/friends in the past 12 months, and this remains the "top travel activity year on year" (YouGov Global Travel & Tourism Whitepaper, 2023, India sample: 1,007 respondents). The EY India "Great Indian Traveller" report (2024) notes that "young Indians often travel with friends or spouses, while older generations prefer family trips." [Global data] A Topdeck Travel Survey found that 40% of millennials globally prefer to travel with friends.

### India domestic group travel volume

Domestic tourist visits reached **2,509.63 million in 2023** (provisional, Ministry of Tourism India), surpassing the pre-pandemic record of 2,322 million in 2019. India's Package Holidays market stood at **USD 11.45 billion in 2025**, with user penetration at 7.9% expected to grow to 13.6% by 2030 (Statista Market Forecast). India's travel tech startup funding hit **USD 600 million across 29 deals in 2024**, a 182% year-on-year jump (Inc42, 2024).

## 2. Nine traveller segments: what data exists and what doesn't

### Framing data on who Indians travel with

The Thomas Cook India/SOTC Holiday Report 2025 (2,500+ respondents) provides the broadest India-specific companion-composition data: **65% prefer multigenerational family travel** (the leading segment), **60% travel as couples**, **28% travel with "frolleagues"** (friends who are colleagues), and just 10% travel solo. A contrasting dataset from Niyo's Annual Travel Report 2025 (1M+ outbound Indian travellers) shows a different picture for outbound travel: **solo travellers at 63.8%**, couples at 19.93%, families at 12.26%, and groups at 4.01% — reflecting the younger, metro, digital-first skew of outbound travellers.

The Airbnb India 2025 Winter Travel Survey (2,155 respondents) found approximately **50% travel with a partner/spouse, one-third with friends, and 30% with multigenerational families**. The ACKO/YouGov Travel Index 2023 (1,000 respondents aged 24–45 across 7 Indian cities) confirmed that most travellers preferred holidays with "friends, spouses, partners, or immediate family."

### (a) Friends — mixed boys and girls group

No India-specific survey isolates mixed-gender friend groups. The closest data: **48% of Indian travellers planned trips with friends** post-vaccination (MakeMyTrip, 2021), the "frolleagues" segment stands at 28% (Thomas Cook/SOTC, 2025), and "groups" account for 4.01% of outbound trips (Niyo, 2025). EY India (2024) describes Gen ACE (under 40) as "adventurous, celebratory, and exploratory, often traveling with peers and influenced by social media." Social media is the #1 source of travel inspiration for Indian travellers at **64%** (American Express Global Travel Trends, 2024, India sample: 1,005 adults). Long weekends and mini-cations are the primary windows, with **47% intending to leverage long weekends/public holidays** (Thomas Cook/SOTC, 2025).

### (b) Boys-only friend trips

**No quantitative data exists for this segment in any accessible Indian travel survey.** Despite the deep cultural association of bachelor trips with destinations like Goa, Manali/Kasol, Ladakh, and Bangkok, no industry report (Booking.com, Thomas Cook, MakeMyTrip, OYO, or others) breaks out "boys-only" or "male friend group" as a distinct segment. This segment is culturally prominent but statistically invisible.

### (c) Girls-only friend trips

This segment has stronger data, though most conflates solo female travel with women-only group travel. The **NCL/OnePoll Survey (January 2024, ~1,000 Indian women)** found that **90% had taken a girls' trip in the past**, **44% intended to travel with female friends in 2024**, and **89% agreed that all-girls getaways are a fantastic way to celebrate friendships.** Motivations included escaping the daily grind (48%), trying new foods (32%), and delving into new experiences (43%).

On the solo women side, **Zostel (2025)** recorded 92,192 solo female bookings, nearly tripling from 33,357 in 2018. Scapia Platform (2025) reported solo travel among women growing **9x year-on-year**. **Airbnb India (2025)** specifically noted that "duo travel followed by small group trips remained the most popular trip type amongst Indian women travellers." Organised women-only travel companies (WOW Club, Wovoyage, Jugni, F5 Escapes, Women on Clouds) form a growing market. Safety remains the #1 concern: [Global data] women spend 20% more on comprehensive travel insurance than men (Mastercard, 2024).

### (d) Mixed friends + spouses (couples friend groups travelling together)

**No specific data found for this segment in India.** No survey or industry report breaks out "couples travelling with other couple friends" as a distinct category. Thomas Cook/SOTC (2025) reports couples at 60% and frolleagues at 28%, but these are not combined. This behaviour likely exists within broader "group travel" and "couples travel" categories but is not independently measured.

### (e) Boys-only friends + spouses and (f) Girls-only friends + spouses

**No data exists for either of these hyper-specific configurations** in any accessible Indian travel research. This is expected given that even broader categories like boys-only trips and mixed-friends-plus-spouses lack quantitative data.

### (g) Family + elders (multigenerational family travel)

This segment has the strongest India-specific data. The **Booking.com Gen.Voyage! Research (2024, 8,000+ travellers across 11 APAC countries, including India)** found:

- **57% of Indian families plan intergenerational trips** (grandparents + parents + children + extended relatives) within the next year
- **51%** emphasise bonding and creating lasting memories during multigenerational trips
- **35%** believe intergenerational holidays make travel affordable by sharing costs
- Millennials lead planning (**48%**); Gen Z co-plans (**47%**); Baby Boomers take a backseat (**23%**)
- **79% of Indian families** have either completed or are planning "skip-gen trips" (grandparents travelling with grandchildren without parents) — the fastest-growing travel segment in APAC (Hilton 2026 Travel Trends Report)

### (h) Family + kids

The **Booking.com Summer 2024 survey** found that **over 50% of Indian families** would allocate travel budget to childcare/travel nannies on trips (vs. 31% global average). 33% of flight searches from Asia include at least one child (Booking.com, 2024). India-specific family travel is strongly domestic-focused, with school holiday calendars as the primary timing driver. EY India (2024) notes "older travellers are more likely to book well ahead, valuing amenities like breakfast and family-friendly activities."

### (i) Family + both elders and kids

Data overlaps significantly with Segment (g). The 57% of Indian families planning intergenerational trips explicitly includes grandparents, parents, children, and extended relatives (Booking.com, 2024). Thomas Cook/SOTC's 65% multigenerational figure covers the full three-generation configuration. Trip duration sweet spot is **4–6 days** for multigenerational groups (Booking.com APAC data, 2024). Hotels are preferred by 49% of such families; resorts by 36%.

## 3. Pain points across the group travel journey

### Pre-trip: the planning gauntlet

The Skyscanner India Travel Trends 2024 report (OnePoll x Skyscanner, 19,000 global respondents including India) provides the most direct India-specific evidence: **52% of Indian travellers said finalising a destination is their primary hurdle** when planning group trips, **48% said deciding travel dates**, and **45% said agreeing on expected costs**. An additional finding: **81% of Indian travellers have taken a group trip to celebrate a birthday or anniversary**, underscoring how common group travel is.

For multigenerational Indian families, the Booking.com Gen.Voyage! study (2024) documented specific friction: **31% face clashing interests and activity preferences**, **32% struggle balancing relaxation vs adventure**, **28% find it difficult keeping children engaged while also engaging older adults**, **27% report generational-gap conflicts**, and **27% struggle with differing energy levels/stamina**.

[Global data] The Experian survey (June 2025, 700+ U.S. adults) found that **"having different budget ranges" is the #1 financial challenge** when travelling with friends, cited by **35% of millennials**. Only **1 in 4 travellers** set a budget upfront. Over half of Gen Z/millennials report money-related disagreements during friend travel; **1 in 5 have ended a friendship** over a money issue during travel.

On **planning fatigue**, the Google/Kantar "Travel Rewired" study (2025, 1,000 Indian respondents aged 18–45+) found that Indian travellers use **7 touchpoints on average** for travel research and planning. **44% of novice travellers find planning overwhelming.** An Amadeus/Microsoft study (2023, survey countries including India) found that only **11% of leisure travellers** did not experience frustration when planning online. **67% of surveyed Indian travellers were first-time travellers or planners** (Google/Kantar, 2025), meaning the majority face a steep learning curve in organising trips.

### During trip: coordination and expense friction

Expense management during group trips is a documented pain point. [Global data] Experian (2025) identified the **top two financial stress points** as "unexpected costs during trips" and "splitting costs fairly when friends have different spending priorities." Young travellers aged 18–34 make up **68% of bill-splitting app users** (AvantStay, 2026 industry data). [Global data] Booking.com (2024, 27,730 respondents) found **55% would like no plans set in stone**, and **69% prefer travelling with loose plans** — suggesting rigid itineraries create friction in groups. No India-specific survey data was found on expense-splitting friction during trips, though the prevalence of Splitwise in India (estimated ~4.5 million Indian users, with Paytm integration) is circumstantial evidence of the pain point.

### Post-trip: settlement and fatigue

[Global data] The Klook Travel Pulse study (2025, 7,000+ respondents across 14 markets including India) found **92% of millennial and Gen Z travellers are hit by post-travel blues**, and nearly **40% immediately start planning their next getaway**. No India-specific quantified data on post-trip expense settlement friction was found; this remains a notable data gap.

### Special focus: social media research and the expectation vs reality gap

Evidence is strong that Indian travellers rely heavily on social media for travel research. YouTube is the dominant platform: **68% of Indian travellers use YouTube for inspiration** (Google/Kantar, 2025). **59% describe creators as "very influential"** in their planning. Social media overall was cited by **64% of Indian travellers** as their #1 inspiration source (American Express, 2024). The Klook/GWI study (2025, 14 markets including India) found **79% of millennial and Gen Z travellers say social media recommendations influence how they plan trips**.

On disappointment, direct India evidence is limited but exists. An academic study by Dr. Ameya Jani et al. (ITM University, IRJHIS journal, July 2024, target sample 500 respondents) confirmed that social media creates an "Instagram vs Reality" phenomenon for Indian travellers, with the portrayal of destinations leading to "heightened expectations, resulting in either enhanced satisfaction or disappointment depending on the actual travel experience." [Global data] A Bitkom study (2025) found **49% of respondents said a destination did not live up to social media images**. A Passport Photo Online/PhotoAid compilation (2026) found **59% of social media users felt duped by a blogger's travel advice** at least once, and **25% cited "accuracy of information" as their top concern** about influencer recommendations.

## 4. Existing solutions and the group planning gap

### Every Indian OTA is built for individuals, not groups

All six major Indian OTAs — **MakeMyTrip** (60%+ market share, ~36M monthly visits, USD 9.8B gross bookings in FY2025), **Goibibo**, **Cleartrip** (Flipkart-owned), **Yatra**, **EaseMyTrip**, and **ixigo** (150M+ users) — are transactional booking platforms optimised for individual users. None offers collaborative itinerary planning, group voting on destinations/dates/activities, or shared expense tracking. MakeMyTrip's Group Desk is the sole exception, but it serves **corporate MICE events** via agent-mediated, managed services — not self-service group planning for friends or families.

### Global planning tools lack India integration

**Wanderlog** offers real-time collaborative itinerary editing and budget tracking but has no India-specific features (no train booking, no INR-specific tools). **TripIt** (20M+ global users) allows itinerary sharing but is "designed for individual travellers, not groups — no shared planning, no accommodation coordination" (Avosquado comparison). **Troupe**, the most purpose-built group planning tool (with ranked-choice voting on destinations/dates), integrates only with Vrbo and lacks any Indian booking platform integration. **Tripoto** (10M+ users), India's largest travel blog community, focuses on inspiration and discovery but offers no collaborative planning features.

### Expense tools solve one piece, not the whole puzzle

**Splitwise** has an estimated **~4.5 million Indian users** and supports Paytm integration — but it is purely a money-tracking app with no itinerary, booking, or coordination features. **Google Sheets** is widely used for manual group planning but requires building everything from scratch and offers no structure, booking integration, or expense management. **WhatsApp** (**853.8 million Indian users**, 2024) is the de facto coordination tool — Indian travellers prefer it **3:1 over email** for travel updates (CampaignHQ/PhocusWire) — but it provides no structured planning, no voting, and information gets buried in chat history.

### The fragmentation tax on group travellers

The documented pattern is that Indian group travellers use a minimum of **5–6 separate tools per trip**: WhatsApp (coordination) + Google Sheets/Docs (planning) + OTA (booking) + Splitwise (expenses) + Google Maps (navigation) + Instagram/YouTube (discovery). No single platform bridges these functions. The Skyscanner 2024 finding that **52% struggle with destination finalisation** directly maps to this gap — no Indian tool offers group voting or collaborative decision-making.

### Eight unaddressed gaps in the India market

The research reveals these specific, documented gaps: (1) No unified group planning + booking platform with Indian integrations (IRCTC trains, MakeMyTrip, RedBus); (2) No collaborative decision-making tool for informal groups — despite 52% citing destination finalisation as a hurdle; (3) No integrated expense tool with UPI payment rails — despite **61% of Indian travellers using UPI** (VIDEC, 2023); (4) No group accommodation coordination (bedroom assignments, check-in logistics) for the growing villa-stay market; (5) No mobile-first group planning for India's overwhelmingly mobile user base (**80%+ of travel bookings happen on mobile**, VIDEC 2023); (6) No vernacular language support in planning tools for Tier-II/III users — despite **48% of Goibibo's new users** coming from these markets; (7) No solution to the "trip organiser burden" where one person ends up managing all logistics; (8) No pipeline from social media discovery (YouTube/Instagram) to collaborative group planning.

## Hypothesis assessment

### H1: People travelling with friends (and their spouses), aged 28–37, face issues agreeing on where to travel, budget, and what to explore

**Verdict: Strongly supported by evidence, though no single India-specific study isolates the exact 28–37 age band or the friends-plus-spouses configuration.**

The strongest direct evidence is the **Skyscanner India 2024 survey**, which found **52% of Indian travellers cite destination finalisation as their primary group trip hurdle**, **48% cite date agreement**, and **45% cite cost agreement** — these are India-specific numbers, though not age- or segment-filtered. The **Booking.com Gen.Voyage! 2024 study** (India breakout) found **31% face clashing interests/activity preferences** and **32% struggle balancing relaxation vs adventure** in multigenerational groups — confirming the activity-agreement problem. [Global data] **Experian (2025)** found that among millennials (roughly ages 28–43), **35% cite "different budget ranges" as the #1 challenge** when travelling with friends, over 50% of Gen Z/millennials have had money disagreements, and only 25% set a budget upfront. The **EY India report (2024)** segments Gen ACE (under 40) as distinct from Gen LUX (over 40), noting the younger cohort travels with peers — aligning with the target demographic. The **friends-plus-spouses configuration specifically** has no data in any Indian survey; this is a critical gap. However, the general friend-group and millennial-age evidence consistently and strongly supports the hypothesis that destination, budget, and activity agreement are major friction points for this cohort.

### H2: The same people face issues researching extensively, rely on social media, and are disappointed upon arrival (expectation vs reality gap)

**Verdict: Partially supported — strong evidence for social media reliance and research fragmentation in India; moderate evidence for disappointment, primarily from global data.**

**Social media reliance is firmly established for India.** The Google/Kantar study (2025, 1,000 Indian respondents) found **68% use YouTube for travel inspiration**, travellers use **7 touchpoints on average**, and **44% of novice travellers find planning overwhelming**. The American Express survey (2024, 1,005 Indian adults) found **64% cite social media as their #1 inspiration source**. The Klook/GWI study (2025, 14 markets including India) found **79% of millennial/Gen Z travellers say social media influences their planning**. The Skyscanner 2025 Smarter Summer Report found **49% of Indian travellers had not finalised holiday plans** and **38% were still uncertain about where to go** — suggesting research does not resolve confusion efficiently.

**The expectation-vs-reality gap has India-specific academic confirmation** but lacks large-scale India survey data. The IRJHIS journal study (Dr. Ameya Jani et al., ITM University, July 2024) confirmed that social media creates "Instagram vs Reality" for Indian travellers, with heightened expectations leading to either "enhanced satisfaction or disappointment." [Global data] The Bitkom study (2025) found **49% say destinations did not live up to social media images**. The Passport Photo Online compilation (2026) found **59% felt duped by blogger travel advice** and **25% cited accuracy concerns** about influencer recommendations. The Talker Research study found **47% of Gen Z** say influencer content contributed to travel-related inadequacy. These global findings likely apply to Indian millennials given their documented high social media reliance, but the absence of a large-scale India-specific disappointment survey prevents a fully confirmed verdict.

## Conclusion: what the evidence actually shows

The Indian group travel market sits at an inflection point — domestic tourist visits have surpassed pre-pandemic records, **88% of Indian travellers are eager to travel** (Google/Kantar, 2025), and **65% prefer travelling with multigenerational families** while **48% plan trips with friends** (Thomas Cook/SOTC, 2025; MakeMyTrip, 2021). Yet the infrastructure for group planning has not kept pace. The three most-cited group planning hurdles — destination, date, and cost consensus — remain unaddressed by any India-focused product. Every major OTA is built for solo transactions; every planning tool is either Western-focused or single-purpose.

The data reveals asymmetric visibility across segments: multigenerational family travel and girls-only trips have robust India-specific research, while boys-only trips, mixed-gender friend groups, and the friends-plus-spouses configuration are statistically invisible despite clear cultural significance. For product builders, the **28–37 friend-and-spouse cohort represents a data desert with strong circumstantial evidence of unmet need** — the Skyscanner and Experian findings on group friction apply squarely to this demographic, but no survey has directly studied them. The social media reliance-to-disappointment pipeline is a documented phenomenon in India at the academic level, though it awaits large-scale quantitative validation. The clearest market signal may be the simplest: Indian group travellers currently need **5–6 separate tools** to plan a single trip, and WhatsApp — a messaging app — remains the closest thing to a group travel coordinator in the country.
